Colleges across the country have come under scrutiny for their responses to sexual violence, but Penn has been relatively unscathed in recent years. However, a number of incidents over the past 40 years have stirred passions and spurred changes in the way the University addresses sexual violence. The Daily Pennsylvanian has compiled a timeline of watershed moments that have forced the Penn community to face the issue of sexual violence head-on.

1973

Sit-in Leads to the Creation of the Penn Women’s Center

After a series of rapes rattled campus, a group of women staged a sit-in on then-President Martin Meyerson’s College Hall office in April 1973. They lobbied for increased security, better lighting throughout campus, rape victim advocates and a space for women, among other things.

After four days, the protesters won. The University began to make changes to make campus safer, and in October 1973, the Penn Women’s Center opened.

1983

Gang Rape Ignites Firestorm on Campus

In February 1983, an alleged gang rape at the Alpha Tau Omega fraternity house sparked an uproar on campus. A female student said that she was carried upstairs by five or six men and raped. After an investigation, the University revoked ATO’s charter, only to have that ruling challenged in a year-long legal battle. After the courts mandated the University conduct a second investigation, the final punishment for the fraternity was a few months barred from campus — a result that many saw as a mere slap on the wrist. “I’m a little disappointed that the sanctions are not as stringent as I would have liked,” then-President Sheldon Hackney said at the time.

1989

‘Women’s Army’ Spray Paints Anti-Rape Graffiti

The campus tensions came to a head in 1989, when students calling themselves the “Women’s Army” spray-painted graffiti overnight reading “a woman was raped here” in several areas across campus, including in front of ATO.

The group’s statement, given anonymously to the DP, stressed the rise of acquaintance rape across campus and said their aim was to “promote dialogue at Penn and to point out that … rape is not acceptable.”

1994

'Take Back the Night' First Held At Penn

Students organized the first Take Back the Night demonstration on campus in 1994, which drew a crowd of over 200 to protest against sexual violence. Students first tried to hold Take Back the Night in 1989, but were unsuccessful due to hecklers. Throughout the 1990s, the event drew large crowds, but also some controversy — critics said the event excluded and antagonized men.

1995

Man Apologizes for Rape at 'Take Back the Night'

During the “speak-out” portion of Take Back the Night — where victims of sexual violence tell their stories — a man went up to the microphone to apologize for raping someone. Organizers condemned the action, calling it a misuse of the event and traumatizing for women trying to heal after being sexually assaulted. The event sparked several years of debate over how involved men should be at Take Back the Night.

1997

Penn Sued Over Its Handling of Campus Rape

In 1997, a former student sued Penn for allegedly covering up her rape three years earlier. The suit, which was settled out of court, claimed that she was raped in what is now called Harrison College House and that Penn Police did not provide her proper support. She also claimed that the University did not include the rape in the crime statistics it reported to the police and federal government — the 1994 crimes report said there were no rapes on campus that year.

However, a 1994 article in the Daily Pennsylvanian reported that a rape had occurred on the day the student claimed she was raped, even quoting Penn police officials referencing the incident. A later, unrelated Department of Education investigation faulted the University for not including a rape in its 1994 annual statistics.

2002

Penn Professor Allegedly Rapes Woman in His Office

Former neurosurgery professor Tracy McIntosh was sentenced to three and a half to seven years in prison in February 2008 for the sexual assault of his college roommate’s niece in his University office.

According to a Daily Pennsylvanian article, McIntosh took the then-23 year old woman out to several bars around campus in September 2002. After she began vomiting, the article said, he took her back to his office in Hayden Hall and had sex with her. She filed a complaint with the University two months later and alerted the police. He was charged with rape in May 2003.

The victim also filed suit with the University, claiming that McIntosh’s colleagues knew he had a history of using his position to harass women and that the University had hindered the investigation. The suit reached a confidential, out-of-court settlement.

2009

String of Rapes

In fall 2009, a string of rapes reported on campus, including two in fraternity houses, forced the topic back to forefront of campus dialogue. Twice during that semester, student anti-violence groups hosted “speak-outs” at the Penn Women’s Center to encourage discussion about the incidents.

“We really have a tragic opportunity with what’s happening to create some kind of momentum to make this a campus-wide issue that’s taken seriously,” Jason Pollack, then-College senior and president of all-male violence prevention group One in Four, said, according to a DP article about the speak-out.

2013

40th Anniversary of the Women's Center

This year, the Penn Women’s Center celebrates the 40th anniversary of its founding. While it initially did not have a building to itself, since 1996 it has occupied a prominent house on Locust Walk.

“We’re doing interviews right now as part of our 40th celebrations, and over and over our interviewees describe PWC as ‘home,’” Women’s Center Director Felicity Paxton said. “That’s really crucial. Advancing gender equity is important, serious and often hard work; it is vital for us to create a space that is welcoming and nurturing.”
